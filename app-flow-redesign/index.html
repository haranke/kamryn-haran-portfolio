myStop App Redesign

A UX Case Study

By Kamryn Haran

Last Updated: January 29, 2022

Overview

myStop is an application used widely around James Madison University and Harrisonburg by students and local residents. It’s an application with real-time bus information that helps students get around campus and Harrisonburg. It offers bus routes, bus stop locations, and times of arrival and departure. The daily use of this app makes effective usability crucial for its users so they can be successful getting to their locations safely and on time.

As an everyday user of myStop I have noticed some features on this application that need repairs to make it more user friendly and helpful. I conducted a survey for users like myself who have also been dealing with frustrations from the app to gather more insight as to what other difficulties they have faced while using myStop. From the research I have gathered, majority of the users have difficulties figuring out what bus lines they should take. This resulted in putting my main focus on how to redesign the Trip Planning feature on the app.

User Research

Design Question: How can we improve the experience of myStop users to allow them to identify what bus line they should take to get to their desired location?

The research I have collected was from 12 myStop users via social media, who were asked to take my Google survey. The survey contained 6 questions about their experiences using the application.

Current User Flow

The main page of myStop is a map of Harrisonburg that you can freely move around. It is stated in the welcome message to select routes that best fit your needs, but not all users know what exact route to select. This leads them to choose the Trip Planning option. When this is clicked on, the app brings you to a page where you can input your to and from locations to get directions. When your information is put in, you are redirected outside of the app to Google Maps on google.com. As shown on the far right image, there are no bus routes listed. This is a very problematic feature on the app because it leads the user no where and still questioning what route they should take.

Pain Points

Pain Point #1: Users have experienced difficulties and confusion while using the myStop app.

It was found that 83.3% of users have experienced difficulties while using the myStop. While only 16.7% users have not. (Percentages from user research). Users are struggling to manage how to work the application because the layout is not user-friendly and has non-working features. It was mentioned multiple times from my survey that myStop is confusing because of the features it contains. Here are a few quotes I have taken from the users that took my survey:

“The directions feature is confusing. It doesn’t always work. “

“The layout is confusing.”

Pain Point #2: myStop relies on Google maps for directions that are not accurate.

For users who are new to myStop they would typically select the Trip Planning button because they don’t know what route to select. When the Trip Planning option is selected, the user is redirected to Google Maps that shows them driving directions, walking directions as well as no bus routes (shown on current user flow images above). There are no bus routes available since myStop routes are not connected to Google Maps. The integration of Google Maps is useless to the user because they do not need driving or walking directions since their intentions for using the app is to take a bus. This is another quote I have received from my survey from a myStop user:

“myStop shows the routes of the buses but sends you to Google maps when planning a trip. Google maps doesnt have the routes. It doesnt work. It just tells you to walk.”

Solutions for Pain Points:

  • Creating a simple navigation system that will allow users to input their to and from locations to get the correct bus route they need to take.
  • Removing myStop’s integration of Google Maps completely.
  • Choosing a bus route(s) will lead users back to the map homepage with the routes highlighted.

The Redesign

I created a solution for the problems myStop users have been facing to make the user flow easier and more user-friendly. My redesign focuses on helping those who use myStop to find out what bus routes they should be taking for their desired locations.

Rationales

When creating my redesign for myStop I thought to begin with changing the homepage to make it easier to navigate the app for first time users. I changed the welcome message to inform users that they can begin with finding their route rather than only mentioning selecting routes to see real-time transit data (stated in the current welcome message). I feel as though the current welcome message is not informative on what to do on the app. On the homepage I also changed the Trip Planning option to Find Your Route which will direct all users to help them find their ideal bus route for any location.

After selecting Find Your Route you will be led to a page to input your to and from addresses, similar to the current design on myStop, I changed the order of “to and from” as well as changing the button to “Get Your Route” for better user flow. I also believe having “to” before “from” is more logical than having “from” first.

The Find Your Route page is followed by the Your Routes page that lists the routes that the user can take for their locations. When the circles located on the right side are selected, a check mark will appear and the user will know which routes they have selected. The design and format of this page is the same as the list of routes on the Select Routes page. I thought that having this format would be good for continuity throughout the app as well as getting users familiar with how the routes are displayed on the Select Routes page.

Once users select their desired routes and hit done, they will be brought back to the main homepage with the selected routes highlighted. This makes it easier for the user because they are not redirected outside of the application and they will be able to see exactly where the bus is going from their location.
